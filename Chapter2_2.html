The seventh scene, which would have concluded the second act in Siegfried’s version, sees Spitz, presumably aggrieved after the situation with the sack, goading farmers into attacking Hans, but Luise defuses the situation. Removing this scene makes dramatic sense, as it has strong narrative ties to the eliminated fourth scene and is ultimately a diversion from the central plot. Removing the finale of the act required a bit of re-composition, however, and there are two pages added to the end of scene 6 that also contain a few new lines of dialogue. As of yet, no certain authorship for this new material can be determined, but it must have been done at Mahler’s behest. Regardless, this is an excellent example of Mahler’s dramatic sense, choosing to end the act with Luise’s pledge to remain faithful to Hans in his absence instead of an aborted lynching. The innkeeper is determined to be rid of Hans after the episode with the sack in scene 4, and has gathered up the community farmers to lynch him under the assumption that he is in league with the devil, all of which seems contrived to get the chorus of farmers on stage for the finale. Hans and Spitz have an argument, during which the farmers attempt to kill Hans, viewing his bag of gold as evidence of his satanic alliance. Luise, however, intercedes on his behalf and convinces them to leave. Mahler’s alternate finale is much more intimate and emotional in concept. Ending the act by depicting the beginning of Hans’s salvation instead of an unnecessary scene that has no real consequence for the narrative makes much more sense for the opera’s plot. An added benefit of both of these cuts (scenes 4 and 7) is a certain homogenization of tone. The fourth scene itself is little more than a comic interlude, complete with wide-leaping vocal acrobatics for the Innkeeper, and the quasi-comic confrontation between Spitz, his farmer-gang, and Hans in the seventh scene interrupts an otherwise heartfelt episode that contains some of the opera’s more beautiful music (elements of which Mahler incorporated into the recomposed finale).

There is enough circumstantial evidence to speculate about when Mahler made these changes during the course of preparation for the production. Scene 7 is left intact in the vocal score, and Mahler even went so far as to mark in all of his cues for the singers, insert rehearsal numbers, and indicate his ideas about stressed syllables. He must have decided to abandon scene 7 very early in rehearsals, however, because the orchestral score has been left almost entirely unedited. There are just a few markings, including a fermata, a handful of cues, a few expressive marks, and most interestingly, one of the only instances of reorchestration in the entire opera, although it was evidently not retained. Mahler writes “Ob Fag” (“oboes/bassoons”) with a rhythm and unstaved pitches next to it, suggesting he had an idea, but no such corrections appeared in the players’ parts (and in any case the scene went unperformed). However, it is valuable since it reveals that Mahler was dissatisfied with the music in this scene, which, when coupled with its weak dramaturgy, made it an obvious early choice for the chopping block. Mahler also prepared the vocal score of the brief scene 4 for rehearsal with the same sorts of indications as found in scene 7. Judging by the conductor’s score, the fourth scene probably survived longer into the rehearsal process than the seventh did, as the score of the fourth scene is marked up to a noticeably greater degree with cues and reminders of metrical changes.

Structural Changes: Act III

Mahler revised the third act finale and the music leading up to it so extensively as to fundamentally change the scene, and did so in the vocal score, which represents the first stage of his editing process. Mahler deleted several lines of dialogue by scratching them out with a pencil and changed others with the result of increased clarity of plot. The first of these major cuts begins in the fourth bar of rehearsal 113, in the finale of the third act (referenced in the table of cuts as III.5.1). The cut material is another encounter between Hans and the now borderline hysterical innkeeper about the attempted theft of Hans’ bag. This cut is logical since the subplot was eliminated in Act II, as described above.

This next cut (III.5.4), beginning at the end of the page in Image V and lasting 9 bars, removes a portion of Luise’s text that contains religious content (a prayer to the angels), a passage which Cosima considered “the heart of the work.”25 The subsequent cut (III.5.5), which begins at the end of page 176 and removes some seven pages of material, also excises lines with allusions to angels and God. After this final cut, the bass and cello line is recomposed to be simpler, but the concluding pages play out as Siegfried originally wrote them. As previously noted, Mahler’s removal of all of the opera’s religious content was the change that incensed the Wagners most. According to Cosima, Siegfried had lobbied to retain “the most important passage.” It must have been the opera’s conclusion, as no other religious scenes remain.

Though some of the cuts in the third act finale can be traced to the earliest stages of Mahler’s interaction with the vocal score, the targeted trimming of sacred components does not seem to have been the motivation. Why Mahler decided to remove all of it is less clear. Considering his overall push for concision and consistency in the opera, it is possible that he chose to eliminate the prayers because piety had not been a thread in the narrative. These aesthetic ideals may not necessarily be what comes to mind when considering Mahler’s own symphonies, given their length and episodic, rapidly shifting forms, but it is worth noting that they are also highly motivic, with a handful of germinal ideas generating much of the music. Many of those motives have been ascribed programmatic meaning by listeners (the descending second of the Ninth Symphony as “Lebwohl,” for instance), resulting in a framing of his works in dramatic terms, despite Mahler’s refusal to provide programs and relatively strong stance on the matter.

Certain musical moments in his symphonies were characterized very explicitly (albeit unofficially) in relation to something extramusical. Bruno Walter relates Mahler’s description of an early part of the Third Symphony as portraying the granite cliffs surrounding the Attersee, where the work was composed.26 The famous third movement funeral march of the First Symphony is well-known as depicting the woodcut of the hunter’s funeral procession by Moritz von Schwind. The opening tenorhorn solo of the Seventh Symphony was described by Mahler as “nature calling like a stag.”27 Beyond mere associations, though, Mahler’s First, Second, and Third Symphonies were each initially given programs that the composer later retracted. Though it does not have a “program” in a literal sense, Mahler’s Sixth Symphony operates similarly; there are themes assigned to individuals (Alma, for instance) and the famous hammer-blows in the final movement are the blows which fell the hero. In the Eighth Symphony’s second part, Mahler actually sets the final scene of Goethe’s Faust, and his early cantata Das Klagende Lied is almost an unstaged opera. This is not to ascribe programs to Mahler’s symphonies, as this was something he vehemently rejected. Nevertheless his lengthy compositions, filled with recurring motives, recognizable topics (cowbells, elements of vernacular music, hammer blows) and a sense of having journeyed somewhere, invite audiences to listen as if it were program music, superimposing their own programs, or programs they have stumbled across (the Seventh as a gradual progression from night to day, for instance) onto Mahler’s abstract works.

When making musical decisions, Mahler spoke more about the effect those choices would have on an audience’s emotions than in technical musical terms, revealing some of the same underlying concerns displayed in his editing – namely, that quality and intensity of affect are of the utmost importance. As an example, the October 1900 performance of the Second Symphony in Munich included less than half the string players Mahler desired. He claimed, “when [I have the forces I want], people will be amazed to see how much it magnifies the effect!”28 Outside of his work conducting his own music, things were little different. Though his alterations to the orchestration of Bärenhäuter are limited, their presence reminds us that Mahler was keenly aware of orchestral timbre as a vehicle for affect (a point agreed upon by both his supporters and detractors in Vienna, to their own ends). In reducing the scope of Siegfried’s opera by removing secondary elements, Mahler was able to concentrate it in the process, staying true to his aesthetic principles by increasing unity and the intensity of affect.


Local Changes

The same aesthetic ideals are evident in localized, minute changes as well, as explored below in an excerpt from Act I, scene 4. In the vocal score, Mahler indicated early versions of some cuts that would become more refined in the final score. The first of these is made one measure before rehearsal 90 and runs until one measure after rehearsal 93 (nineteen measures). This scene depicts Hans playing dice with the Stranger for possession of the devil’s souls that Hans has been instructed to guard. The Stranger continually outplays Hans, and each time they increase the stakes. Mahler apparently felt that this was going on too long and trimmed the penultimate game and the corresponding dialogue so that they immediately raise the wager to all of the souls. The precise placement of the cut was probably determined by the text; as it stands in the vocal score, Mahler cuts from “oder hätte ich zu wählen” to “so gält es nun alle Seelen, ” surgically removing music in such a way that it required no recomposition, either of text or music, and it even rhymes the same word with “wählen” as Siegfried does in his original version. The resultant of this cut is a bit leaner dramatically, and faster paced.

The final draft of this moment in the full score, however, has a substantially more complex reworking. Starting a bar before rehearsal 89 and lasting for three pages, the original score has new pages pasted over it, in an unknown copyist’s hand. The last of these added pages contains the “oder hätte ich zu wählen” line noted above, but now the cut removes that line from Mahler’s added pages. The “de” end of the cut (“vi-de”) arrives just before an identical line on the next page in the original engraved score, with Mahler’s old cut from the vocal score (described in the preceding paragraph) removed: the wager is again 500 souls, as in Siegfried’s unaltered libretto, instead of jumping immediately to all of them. Evidently Mahler decided that his initial impulse didn’t work, and so he reversed his decision. A few bars later there is another cut (I.4.4) just after the dice roll for the 500 souls, which removes most of the dialogue that does not directly advance the plot, and picks up again just before the next dice roll, which is for all the souls.

Seeing the relatively complicated evolution of this single minor cut reveals the depth of thought and effort that Mahler was lavishing on this score. Despite producing no mature theatrical works himself, his many years as an opera director had made him a man of the theatre. He perceived the drama to be impeded by superfluous dialogue between games, and tested different ways of removing the offending material before settling on one that makes the resultant dramaturgy more concise and effective.


Part III: Mahler the Editor — Observations and Deductions

As we have seen, Mahler had a somewhat complex view on editing music. Neither his own scores nor those of other composers were above reproach, and he made changes to both where he thought them warranted. Some of Mahler’s alterations were meant to be permanent revisions to the score, while others were temporary, determined by immediate context. His process was highly dependent on the particular circumstances of a performance, down to the specific venue in some instances. Mahler’s bold decisions regarding symphonic repertoire are well documented. He removed some chords in the trombone parts during rehearsals for a performance of his Second Symphony in 1907, and defended his choice by saying, “Hail to the conductor who in the future will change my scores according to the acoustics of the concert hall.” Given this attention to bespoke individual performances, it isn’t terribly surprising that his protégé and assistant Otto Klemperer later claimed that Mahler viewed such edits as personal changes only, not to be repeated by others without arriving at the same alterations independently. Klemperer believed that the publication of Mahler’s Schumann symphony edits would have greatly upset him, but this has not stopped conductors from performing them.29

Mahler took such editing very seriously throughout his conducting career. He expanded that aspect of his work dramatically during his season leading the subscription concerts in Hamburg in 1894 and 1895, during which time he ceased marking only his scores and began marking the complete set of orchestral parts as well. This was a task he entrusted to no one else, refusing assistance from his colleagues and taking stacks of music home with him to mark in the evenings, taking great pains to ensure that nothing was overlooked by his players.30

Many of the changes he made in repertory works he viewed as undoing the damage done by other conductors’ interpretations in the past, such as the changes in dynamics made in the Schumann and Beethoven symphonies. Mahler judged the worship of tradition antithetical to the art of music, claiming that the stagnant repertoire, lack of innovation, and mediocre and predictable performances were “Schlamperei” (translated loosely, “sloppy”). Thus he set about delivering radically new interpretations of old favorites, which often became a point of contention with complacent musicians. Mahler was aware that some of his revisions would meet with automatic hostility from the antisemitic and deeply conservative audience, and so he explained some of his alterations (for the Beethoven symphonies, for example) in the program notes provided at the concert.31

In the operatic genre, however, Mahler’s changes are somewhat harder to understand. There is no video documentation to reveal whether changes in the score are in any way related to the singers or stage production, for example. Here Mahler’s notions of authenticity were sometimes subjugated in the service of greater dramatic effect. Mahler’s reorchestrations in Beethoven’s Fidelio, made for a 1908 production at the Metropolitan Opera, were judged by reviewers to have increased the emotional and dramatic impact, especially in the introduction to the second act.32 In another instance, Mahler removed the overture to one of Haydn’s operas and replaced it with a Haydn “symphony” (which specific one, or whether it was complete, is not clear), a change which was very well received by Eduard Hanslick.33 Nonetheless, he was strongly opposed to making alterations where he thought them unnecessary, as mentioned earlier with regard to his Viennese performances of many of Richard Wagner’s operas. His judgement alone was the metric.

In addition to the standard operatic fare of the late nineteenth century, Mahler introduced quite a few new works to the Hofoper’s repertoire during his 10-year post there, and some of these works were subject to even greater modification than the music mentioned previously. In fact, the very first new work Mahler gave in Vienna, in the fall of 1897, was Bedřich Smetana’s Dalibor. He partially reorchestrated it and gave it an entirely new finale, suggesting that he found Smetana’s original ending dramatically unsuccessful.34 Nevertheless, he resisted performing works he thought were truly subpar, even when they were by popular composers. His initial refusal to stage Hugo Wolf’s Der Corregidor in 1897 was the end of their relationship; days later Wolf had a psychological breakdown that resulted in his institutionalization.35 The opera administration also attempted to force Mahler to program certain works. Prince and Obersthofmeister Liechtenstein’s attempt to get an opera by Count Géza Zichy staged was doomed by Mahler’s refusal to cooperate despite the potential danger to his career. He declared that he was only beholden to the Emperor, and if he were forced to perform the opera, the posters would have to state, “By Royal Command.” This ended Liechtenstein’s crusade.36 Interestingly, Klemperer indicates that Count Zichy also tried to get Mahler to stage his opera while he was the conductor in Budapest, which accelerated Mahler’s relocation to Hamburg. 37 Siegfried Wagner’s Der Bärenhäuter, however, was one work that he was politically unable to reject despite his low opinion of it (he did not hesitate to decline Siegfried’s subsequent attempts in the genre, however).38 Though hotly anticipated as the debut of Richard Wagner’s son, Viennese audiences quickly reached similar conclusions, and a joke circulated that “the Emperor wants to ennoble Siegfried Wagner because he prefers Siegfried von Wagner to Siegfried Wagner.”39 So it came to be staged in Mahler’s highly modified version.

The precise reasons for each of Mahler’s alterations are naturally impossible to divine with certainty, but with knowledge of Mahler’s editorial tendencies and artistic concerns, as well as what little he said about the opera, it is possible to hazard some reasonably educated guesses. He apparently found the first act fairly effective but took great issue with the remainder of the work, claiming that it hardly seemed to have been composed by the same man.40 As noted, the largest changes he made included striking the fourth and seventh scenes of the second act wholesale, which results in a more cohesive dramatic unity. These scenes about Hans’ conflict with a secondary character are ultimately irrelevant to the dramatic through-line of the work, which is Hans’ eventual redemption by Luise through the power of love and fidelity (a very Wagnerian theme). Though Mahler did not write any operas, making direct comparison impossible, his symphonic compositions have a similar unity and cohesion of theme. This is especially true in the later works like the Ninth Symphony and Das Lied von der Erde, both of which are highly unified by recurring motives, such as the turn and the descending major second in the Ninth.41

The smaller modifications to the Wagner score are predominantly in the form of short cuts and changes to the vocal parts. These also exemplify Mahler’s push for concision and clarity, as he often made changes to spell out plot developments for the audience, or to trim scenes that had overstayed their welcome. This motivation is already well-documented by David Pickett as the root of Mahler’s cuts in the symphonic works of Bruckner and Schubert, which often had repetitious phrases eliminated, though always in such a way that the seams were not audible. 42

Mahler’s operatic revisions seem to generally fall into one or both of two categories: those that improve the work’s focus, and those that heighten dramatic impact. In the case of Der Bärenhäuter, unnecessary scenes for secondary characters and excessive repetition dilute dramatic focus, and the fault lay primarily with the composer’s poorly-edited libretto rather than the score itself. An early example of the latter is in Act I, scene 1 (cut I.1.1), where he cut measures 30-39 of the scene. 43 Soldiers are returning home to the town after fighting in the 30 Years’ War, and the villagers are gathering excitedly to receive them. These nine excised bars consist of exclamations of excitement from the townsfolk (the likes of which we’ve already heard in the previous phrases), and the music is likewise made of the same martial material that immediately precedes it and is of no particular interest. These factors make those nine bars superfluous and therefore prime candidates for removal.

Another instance of a minor change that enhanced dramatic focus by heightening the impact for the audience occurs in Act II, Scene 1. In measures 56 and 57 of that scene, Mahler adds dialogue (with new music) for Anna (“Mannsbilder ihr seid zu feig. Ich selbst will seh’n wer vor die Tür steht.”). Immediately after Anna delivers these lines, Mahler adds a fermata and writes in “aufschrei.”44 The sudden silence in the orchestra broken by the scream must have been startling and certainly makes for a markedly more dramatic scene than the original version Siegfried wrote, but without massive deviation from the letter of the score.

Interestingly, Mahler made very few changes to the dynamics and orchestration of Siegfried’s opera, suggesting that he found the instrumental balances in the work to be at least sufficient. In some cases, Mahler modified the instrumental parts for dramatic reasons. In one instance in the first scene of Act 1 (beginning at rehearsal 4), Mahler adds crescendi in the strings leading up to the end of a phrase, which is followed by a sparser texture, producing a more dramatic climax than Siegfried’s unadulterated version. 45
